Background

What is actually at risk

Most of the cryptography securing digital communications today falls into two families: RSA and elliptic curve cryptography (ECC). RSA is built on the hardness of factoring large integers. ECC is built on the hardness of the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP). Both are used everywhere. I mean everywhere: 

TLS / HTTPS     - the padlock on every website. RSA or ECDH for key exchange,
                  ECDSA for authentication. Every browser, every API call.
SSH             - server authentication and session key establishment.
PGP / GPG       - email encryption and code signing.
Signal / iMessage / WhatsApp - end-to-end encryption via ECDH key agreement.
VPNs            - IPsec and WireGuard both rely on Diffie-Hellman variants.
JWT / OAuth     - RS256 and ES256 are RSA and ECDSA respectively.
Blockchain      - Bitcoin and Ethereum use secp256k1 ECDSA for transaction signing.

Symmetric cryptography (AES, ChaCha20) is not the problem here. That is the encryption that actually protects data in flight. The asymmetric layer sitting underneath it, which handles key exchange and authentication, is where the exposure lives.

Which attacks break them

Shor's algorithm is the main threat. Published in 1994, it runs on a quantum computer and solves both integer factorization and the discrete logarithm problem in polynomial time. That directly breaks RSA and ECC. A classical computer would need longer than the age of the universe to break a 256-bit elliptic curve key. A sufficiently large quantum computer running Shor's algorithm can do it in minutes.

Grover's algorithm is the secondary concern. It provides a quadratic speedup for brute-force search, which effectively halves the security of symmetric keys. AES-128 drops to roughly 64-bit effective security against a quantum adversary. AES-256 is still fine at 128-bit effective security. Symmetric crypto just needs larger key sizes. Asymmetric crypto needs to be replaced entirely.

What the Google paper says

A paper out of Google Quantum AI, released April 1, 2026, gives updated resource estimates for breaking the 256-bit ECDLP over secp256k1, the specific curve used by Bitcoin and Ethereum. The numbers are more concrete and more troubling than previous estimates: 

≤ 1200 logical qubits and ≤ 90 million Toffoli gates, or
≤ 1450 logical qubits and ≤ 70 million Toffoli gates.

On superconducting hardware with 10^-3 physical error rates and
planar connectivity: execution in minutes, using fewer than
500,000 physical qubits.

500,000 physical qubits is not a science fiction number anymore. Google's Willow chip hit 105 qubits in 2024. The trajectory matters more than the current count, and the resource requirements are shrinking faster than most organizations are moving.

The paper also draws a useful distinction between "fast-clock" architectures (superconducting, photonic) and "slow-clock" architectures (neutral atom, ion trap). The first fast-clock cryptographically relevant quantum computer would be capable of "on-spend" attacks: intercepting a public transaction in the mempool and cracking the key fast enough to redirect funds before the transaction confirms. That is not a distant theoretical scenario. It is a specific attack with a specific time window.

The problem that is already happening

Even before a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists, there is a concrete attack already in progress: collect now, decrypt later (also called harvest now, decrypt later, or HNDL). The strategy is simple. Record encrypted traffic today. Store it. Decrypt it when the hardware catches up. Anyone with the storage budget and the motive has already started.

The implications are not hypothetical. Any data transmitted today that needs to stay confidential for more than a decade is already potentially compromised. Medical records. Legal communications. Government intelligence. Financial transactions. Source code. The encryption protecting that data was designed to be hard to break with classical hardware, and that assumption is on a known countdown.

Forward secrecy helps somewhat. TLS with ephemeral Diffie-Hellman means past sessions cannot be decrypted even if the long-term key is later broken. But it only covers the session key, not the authentication step. And plenty of deployed software does not use forward secrecy at all.

Why PQC adoption is not keeping up

NIST finalized its first post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024: ML-KEM (CRYSTALS-Kyber) for key encapsulation and ML-DSA (CRYSTALS-Dilithium) for digital signatures. Both are lattice-based schemes believed to be hard for classical and quantum computers alike. The standards exist. The libraries exist. The path forward is clear.

Adoption is slow anyway. TLS libraries are being updated, but rolling that out across enterprise infrastructure, embedded systems, long-lived hardware, and legacy software takes years. The blockchain situation is particularly rough: migrating a signature scheme on a live decentralized network requires broad consensus under adversarial conditions, and dormant wallets with old keys may never get migrated at all.

The gap between "the standard exists" and "the standard is deployed everywhere it needs to be" is measured in years. The timeline for a quantum computer capable of breaking 256-bit ECC is uncertain, but this paper shows the resource requirements are no longer implausible at current scaling rates. The urgency is not matched by the pace of migration.

Thoughts

What you can actually do

The good news is that the practical steps are well understood. The bad news is that most people have not done them. Here is where I would start depending on your situation: 

If you write or maintain software:
  - Audit your TLS configuration. Make sure you are using ephemeral key
    exchange (ECDHE or DHE). Forward secrecy does not help against HNDL
    entirely, but it limits the blast radius.
  - Swap AES-128 for AES-256 wherever you control the symmetric layer.
    Grover's cuts effective key size in half. AES-256 survives that;
    AES-128 does not comfortably.
  - Start testing ML-KEM and ML-DSA in non-production environments. Both
    are in OpenSSL 3.5 and liboqs. The migration will take time, so
    starting now while there is no pressure is the right call.
  - Avoid generating new long-lived RSA or ECC keys for things that need
    to stay secret for more than a few years.

If you work at an organization handling sensitive data:
  - Do a cryptographic inventory. You probably do not have a clear picture
    of everywhere RSA and ECC are deployed. You need one before you can
    migrate anything.
  - Prioritize data with long sensitivity shelf life: medical records,
    legal documents, anything classified. That is what is most at risk
    from HNDL today.
  - Start migration planning. Enterprise rollouts take years. You want to
    have a plan before the urgency is obvious to everyone else.

If you work in crypto / blockchain:
  - Push for PQC migration discussions in your community. This is harder
    than anywhere else because it requires network-wide coordination, which
    means starting the conversation early is the only realistic path.

For regular people:
  - Signal added post-quantum key agreement (PQXDH) in 2023. Use it.
  - Keep your software updated. PQC support is being rolled out across
    TLS libraries and browsers. Staying current is the easiest way to
    benefit from that work.

The discontinuity nobody is taking seriously

The Google paper uses the word "discontinuity" to describe what a cryptographically relevant quantum computer represents, and I think that framing is exactly right. Most security improvements are incremental. Patch a vulnerability, update a library, rotate a key. A CRQC is not incremental. It is a retroactive break of most of the trust infrastructure the internet has been built on for thirty years, applied simultaneously to everything ever encrypted with RSA or ECC.

Collect now, decrypt later makes it worse because the attack surface is not just future communications. It includes everything ever transmitted. We do not get to decide the threat exists only after the hardware arrives. Adversaries with the resources and the motive to store traffic at scale have been doing that for a long time. The data is sitting there waiting for the key to exist.

The pushback I keep hearing is "quantum computers aren't there yet." Sure, that is true. It is also completely beside the point. The question is not whether your data is being decrypted today. The question is whether data you transmit over the next five to ten years could be decrypted later. For most organizations handling sensitive information, the answer is yes, and the window to act before exposure is roughly the same timescale as the expected arrival of a CRQC.

The path forward is not complicated. NIST gave us the standards. Migrate to ML-KEM and ML-DSA. Prioritize forward secrecy everywhere. Audit what you are transmitting that has long-term sensitivity. The hard part is not technical. It is getting people to treat a threat that has not visibly materialized yet with the seriousness it deserves. We are probably going to wait until something breaks before we take this seriously enough, which is a shame.
